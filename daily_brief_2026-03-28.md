**[GOLD PRICE SNAPSHOT]**

Let's get into the numbers. Gold is trading around $4,430 per ounce as we head into the weekend. Friday's session saw us open near $4,379, grind higher through the European session, and tag a high of $4,475 before pulling back slightly into the close. That's a 1.2% move on the day — and the important part is the character of that move. This wasn't a spike-and-fade. Gold held its gains through the New York session, which tells me there's real demand sitting under this market, not just a headline pop. The intraday range of about $100 — $4,376 to $4,475 — is wider than what we've been seeing, and that's consistent with the elevated ATR regime the system has been flagging all week. When ranges expand like this on up days, it tells you the buyers are in control of the tape.

**[KEY LEVELS]**

On the upside, $4,475 is the immediate resistance — that's where we stalled on Friday. Above that, $4,500 is the big psychological round number and the line in the sand. Multiple technical sources are watching the $4,503 to $4,516 zone as the breakout confirmation area. If gold punches through there with volume, the next magnet is $4,630, which was the March high before the sell-off.

On the support side, $4,380 is the first level I care about — that's roughly where we opened today and where the 50-day moving average is converging. Below that, $4,316 is the 200-day zone, and that's my invalidation line. If gold trades below $4,316 on a daily close, the bullish thesis needs serious re-evaluation. But right now, we're closer to resistance than support, and the trend is pressing higher.

**[DXY & RATES]**

Now here's where it gets interesting. The dollar index, the DXY, closed at 100.21 — up 0.31% on the session and higher for three consecutive days. Over the past month, the dollar has firmed about 1.86%. Normally, a rising dollar is a headwind for gold. But gold rallied 1.2% on Friday despite that dollar strength. That divergence is significant. When gold goes up while the dollar goes up, it's telling you something else is driving the bid — and right now, that something is geopolitical risk.

The 10-year Treasury yield is sitting at 4.43%, near eight-month highs. Yields at these levels should, in theory, be pulling capital away from gold — you're getting paid 4.4% to hold Treasuries, and gold pays you nothing. But again, gold isn't listening to yields right now. It's listening to the Middle East. That divergence from the usual dollar-yield relationship is one of the clearest signals that safe-haven demand is the dominant force in this market.

**[MACRO & NEWS DRIVERS]**

Driver number one: the Middle East conflict. The U.S. and Israel launched joint strikes on Iran's nuclear program in late February. Iran responded with missile and drone strikes against Israel, activated Hezbollah, and closed the Straits of Hormuz. This is not a one-day headline — this is a structural shift in risk. President Trump extended the Iran deal deadline this week, which gave a brief sigh of relief, but the military situation on the ground has not de-escalated. As long as the Straits of Hormuz are under threat, you've got oil price risk feeding into inflation expectations, which keeps gold bid as a hedge.

Driver number two: the dollar tug-of-war. The dollar is strengthening because geopolitical uncertainty is also a dollar positive — the classic flight to the two safe havens simultaneously. But the dollar's strength is being driven more by rate differentials and risk aversion than by economic outperformance. If the conflict escalates further and oil spikes, inflation expectations will force the Fed's hand, and that's when the dollar-gold relationship could flip.

Driver number three: positioning. The latest COT report from March 24th shows managed money net long about 168,000 contracts. That's a healthy long position but not extreme — we've seen it stretch past 200,000 in prior bull runs. Open interest actually declined by about 7,500 contracts, which suggests some profit-taking happened, but the core long position is intact.

**[BIAS & OUTLOOK]**

I'm bullish. Not wildly bullish — the dollar and yields are real headwinds — but the geopolitical bid is the dominant force right now, and it's not going away over a weekend. Gold's ability to rally into $4,475 despite a firming dollar and 4.43% yields tells me the safe-haven demand is genuine and structural, not just a headlines trade.

For the bullish case to play out, I need to see $4,500 break on a daily close with decent volume. If that happens early next week, $4,630 becomes the next target. The invalidation is a daily close below $4,316 — the 200-day moving average zone. If we lose that level, the macro headwinds are winning and I'd step aside.

Timeframe: I'm thinking swing — this is a multi-day to multi-week setup, not an intraday scalp. The geopolitical backdrop gives this trade legs, but you need to give it room to breathe.

**[TODAY I'M WATCHING]**

I'm personally watching the Sunday night open — specifically whether gold gaps above Friday's $4,475 high during the Sydney and Tokyo sessions. A gap-up open with follow-through into the $4,500 handle would tell me the weekend risk premium is expanding, and I'll be watching how the London session responds to that level. If $4,475 gets rejected again, I want to see $4,380 hold as the floor. That's my line. Below $4,380 on a daily close and I start trimming.

**[TRADER SCHOOL]**

If you're newer to this, you've probably heard me say "safe-haven flows" a few times today. Here's what that actually means. When big institutional money gets nervous — war, financial crisis, pandemic — it moves out of risky assets like stocks and into assets perceived as safe stores of value. Gold is the oldest safe haven in the world. The U.S. dollar and Treasury bonds are the others. That's why you sometimes see gold and the dollar rise at the same time, which seems contradictory — but it makes sense when you understand both are absorbing the same fear trade. The key for us is knowing when safe-haven demand is the dominant driver, because when it is, the normal dollar-gold inverse relationship breaks down, and that's exactly what we're seeing right now.
